Protect Yourself from Burns

Posted Feb. 28

According to the American Burn Association, more than 450,000 people in the United States seek medical attention for burns each year. A burn is tissue damage caused by heat, chemicals, electricity, sunlight or radiation.

To protect yourself from burns:

  • Follow your building's fire safety protocol.
  • Ensure that every floor of your home and office building has a smoke alarm.
  • Be cautious around hot foods and liquids.
  • Protect your skin from the sun by wearing protective clothing and applying sunscreen with a sun protection factor of at least 15 at least 20 minutes before exposure.
  • Wear appropriate personal protective equipment when working with chemicals or electricity.

For more information, visit Burns site on the EPA intranet.

Lead an Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle

Posted March 1

Inflammation is your immune system’s response to fight off harmful stimuli, but if the inflammatory response persists for an extended period, the symptoms can become chronic and harm the body. Chronic inflammatory diseases include stroke, heart disease, cancer and diabetes. While more research is needed to fully understand the relationship between inflammation and chronic conditions, the following habits can reduce your risk for chronic inflammation while also benefitting your overall wellbeing:

  • Avoid processed foods and instead eat a healthy diet that includes fruits, vegetables, whole grains and healthy fats (e.g., olive oil, fatty fish).
  • Do not smoke.
  • Maintain a healthy weight.
  • Get regular exercise.
  • Manage stress.

For more information, visit the Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle site on the EPA intranet.

Reduce Your Radon Risk

Posted Feb. 14

Radon is a naturally occurring, odorless gas that can damage your lungs when inhaled over time. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, radon gas exposure is the second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking. For non-smokers, radon exposure is the leading cause of lung cancer.

Radon gas can enter homes and buildings through cracks in floors, walls and foundations. The EPA recommends testing homes for radon and reducing any elevated levels detected (i.e., 4 picocuries per liter or more). If a test shows above-acceptable levels, there are several methods for reducing radon levels in your home such as installing a home ventilation pipe system and fan.

For more information, visit the EPA intranet.

Exercises at Your Desk

Posted Feb. 11

Take a short exercise break during your workday to stay limber, increase productivity, and improve your health. Perform resistance band exercises to work multiple muscle groups at once and increase your coordination. Pilates, strength training, core, and other exercises can increase your flexibility, improve your posture, and maintain a healthy heart. Visit the Exercises at Your Desk page to view videos and get started exercising at your desk.

If you do not have a resistance band and want to try these exercises at your desk, contact your wellness coordinator, SeVera Wilson, at wilson.severa@epa.gov to borrow one.
