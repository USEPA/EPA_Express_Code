EPA Authors with Published Articles Have a Chance to Earn $10K Award

Posted March 13

EPA employees have a chance to earn up to a $10,000 Level I award for their published work as part of the prestigious 2019 Scientific and Technological Achievement Awards (STAA). A Level II award can land authors $5,000 and a Level III award can earn a $2,000 award. The program, managed by the Office of Research and Development, will be open for nominations April 1 through May 1.

Each year, EPA recognizes outstanding papers written by agency staff and published in scientific and technical journals. These peer-reviewed publications represent the quality and depth of EPA science, and they provide foundational support to agency decision-making and advancement of science.

This year's nominations will be accepted via the BAP Provisioning Form located on the the Agency Forms intranet site. To fill out the form, follow the BAP Provision Instructions for STAA.

Learn more about the nomination procedures and guidelines.

Help Spot Heart Attacks

Posted March 7

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, approximately 735,000 Americans have a heart attack every year. Recognizing the signs and symptoms of a heart attack is the best way to prevent cardiac death. Signs and symptoms include the following:

  • Pain or discomfort in the chest or other areas of the upper body (i.e., one or both arms, back, neck, jaw or stomach).
  • Shortness of breath.
  • Nausea or vomiting.
  • Feeling weak, lightheaded or faint.
  • Unexplained or unusual tiredness.

Call 911 immediately if you or someone around you experiences these symptoms.

For more information, visit the EPA intranet.

Be Heart Smart

Posted March 5

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of mortality for both men and women in the United States, responsible for one in three deaths. Although people of all ages and backgrounds can develop heart disease, you can reduce your risk by:

  • Eating a healthy diet.
  • Exercising regularly.
  • Maintaining a healthy weight.
  • Managing stress.
  • Not smoking.
  • Limiting alcohol use.
  • Reducing your exposure to unhealthy air quality.

For more information, visit the EPA intranet.

Learn the Warning Signs of a Stroke

Posted March 4

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, strokes are the fifth leading cause of death in the United States and are a major cause of adult disability. Most strokes occur when blood clots or other particles block the blood vessels to the brain.

Common warning signs and symptoms of a stroke are:

  • Sudden trouble talking or walking.
  • Sudden confusion.
  • Sudden dizziness, or loss of balance or coordination.
  • Sudden numbness in the face, arm or leg, especially on one side of the body.
  • Severe headache with no known cause.

Call 911 immediately if you or someone around you experiences these symptoms.

For more information, visit the EPA intranet.

Protect Yourself from Burns

Posted Feb. 28

According to the American Burn Association, more than 450,000 people in the United States seek medical attention for burns each year. A burn is tissue damage caused by heat, chemicals, electricity, sunlight or radiation.

To protect yourself from burns:

  • Follow your building's fire safety protocol.
  • Ensure that every floor of your home and office building has a smoke alarm.
  • Be cautious around hot foods and liquids.
  • Protect your skin from the sun by wearing protective clothing and applying sunscreen with a sun protection factor of at least 15 at least 20 minutes before exposure.
  • Wear appropriate personal protective equipment when working with chemicals or electricity.

For more information, visit Burns site on the EPA intranet.

Lead an Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle

Posted March 1

Inflammation is your immune system's response to fight off harmful stimuli, but if the inflammatory response persists for an extended period, the symptoms can become chronic and harm the body. Chronic inflammatory diseases include stroke, heart disease, cancer and diabetes. While more research is needed to fully understand the relationship between inflammation and chronic conditions, the following habits can reduce your risk for chronic inflammation while also benefitting your overall wellbeing:

  • Avoid processed foods and instead eat a healthy diet that includes fruits, vegetables, whole grains and healthy fats (e.g., olive oil, fatty fish).
  • Do not smoke.
  • Maintain a healthy weight.
  • Get regular exercise.
  • Manage stress.

For more information, visit the Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle site on the EPA intranet.
