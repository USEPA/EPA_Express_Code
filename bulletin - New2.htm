OIG Helps Address Workplace Violence

Posted Aug. 14

The Office of Inspector General (OIG) helps EPA implement its Preventing Violence in the Workplace policy by investigating allegations of assault or serious threats of violence in the EPA workplace. Anyone who experiences or witnesses workplace violence, as well as any supervisor who receives a report of workplace violence, should immediately:

  • Contact the most readily available law enforcement authority to ensure no further violence occurs.
  • Ensure that any employee who has been assaulted receives medical attention.
  • Notify the OIG Hotline at (888) 546-8740.

The OIG will initiate an investigation when there is a physical assault causing harm or serious bodily injury or when there is a credible threat of imminent harm or serious bodily injury. Generally the OIG will not investigate inappropriate statements and behavior or allegations of workplace harassment and bullying.

For more information, refer to the OIG's fact sheet, contact your management, and/or consult EPA Orders 1400.1 A2 and 4711. For more information about the EPA OIG in general:

  • Visit the OIG website.
  • Subscribe to our OIG email updates.
  • Learn more about our OIG Hotline.
  • Follow us on Twitter @EPAoig.

Feds Feed Families: Feds Feed Families: Donate More Than Just Canned Goods!

Posted Aug. 27

Most people think of canned food when they think of food drives. However, there are many other types of nonperishable items you can bring to support them. For example, you can donate pasta, rice, healthy snacks, juice, cereals, peanut butter, and more. Some food banks will also accept hygiene and household items, such as laundry detergent and diapers. Please check with your local Feds Feed Families coordinator or your local food bank to see if they take these items. Get more information and details from the Feds Feed Families intranet site.

Breastfeeding Your Baby

Posted Aug. 2

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, nearly 52 percent of American women continue to breastfeed their babies six months after birth. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that mothers continue to breastfeed through the first year after birth and use supplemental solid food. Breastfed babies are less likely to have diarrhea, ear infections, or pneumonia; develop asthma or allergies; or become obese. Mothers who breastfeed have a decreased risk of breast and ovarian cancers.

The Headquarters Maternal Wellness Program offers regular prenatal breastfeeding classes and Worksite Brown Bag Lunch Seminars on topics such as weaning and pumping best practices. For more information, please visit the EPA intranet.
